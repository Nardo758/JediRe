The two layers produce fundamentally different investment signals, and the amenity package builder needs both to give real guidance.
Layer 1 (comp parity) is what most platforms attempt. You look at the F6 comp set and count: 8 of 11 comps have a dog park, your building doesn't, comps with dog parks average $45/unit more in rent. This tells you dog park is table stakes — you're losing deals over it. The penetration rate matters: at 30% penetration, an amenity is still a differentiator; at 70%+ it's an arms race and you're behind. The rent lift attribution isolates the amenity's contribution by comparing rents of otherwise-similar comps with and without. All of this comes from apartments.com scraper data and the F6 comp set — data you already collect.
Layer 2 (service desert) is where it gets interesting and where your existing Google Places API integration becomes an intelligence weapon. Here's the methodology:
The Google Places API scan runs a radius query (0.5mi, 1mi, 2mi rings) for specific POI categories: daycare, pet grooming/boarding, coworking spaces, dry cleaning, EV charging, urgent care, pharmacy, grocery/convenience, coffee shops, fitness/yoga studios. For each category, you count the number of providers within each ring and compute a density score normalized against the submarket average. A category with zero providers within a mile is a desert — and a potential monopoly amenity.
But the density alone isn't enough. The demand-supply mismatch layer crosses the POI density against the demographic profile from ArcGIS GeoEnrichment. If the 1-mile demo shows 68% renters, median age 34, median household income $98K, and 72% college-educated — but zero coworking spaces within a mile — that's a screaming signal. Young professionals with remote work capability and no nearby coworking option will pay a premium for in-building coworking. Conversely, if the demo is median age 55 and retired, a coworking space is dead weight.
The NAICS business formation data adds a forward-looking dimension. If healthcare NAICS formations are surging in the submarket (the emerging employment center pattern you already have designed), that tells you healthcare workers are arriving — people who work long shifts and need nearby daycare, not coworking. The demand profile shifts based on who's coming, not just who's there.
The review complaint mining is the highest-signal input of all. The googleReviewIntelligence.service.ts already classifies reviews by category. If residents at surrounding properties are writing "wish there was somewhere to walk the dog" or "no good coffee within walking distance" or "have to drive 20 minutes for daycare" — those are explicit demand signals for services your building could capture. The NLP classification already exists; you just need a "missing services" category tag added to the sentiment classifier.
This changes the amenity package builder tiers:
The current design had three tiers: base, competitive, premium. With the service desert layer, it should be four: base (infrastructure — parking, trash, mail), competitive (match comps — dog park, fitness center, pool), premium (exceed comps — rooftop, concierge, cold storage), and monopoly (fill a desert — daycare, coworking, pet spa, EV charging where none exists nearby). The monopoly tier has fundamentally different economics — you're not competing on price, you're capturing demand that currently has no local supply. That justifies a different rent lift model in the ProForma: competitive amenities add $25-75/unit, monopoly amenities can justify $100-200/unit because the alternative is a 15-minute drive.
For the deal type branching: Development deals get the full builder across all four tiers because you're programming from scratch. Redevelopment deals get the competitive and monopoly tiers — you're looking at what to add, and the desert analysis tells you which additions have the highest ROI. Existing deals get a gap analysis showing which tier each current amenity falls into and where the biggest lifts are available with the lowest capital cost.
