PART 1 — Why stabilization isn't a passive calculation

The stabilization-window math I described isn't pure observation of 
a vacancy trajectory. It's an underwriting decision the agent 
reasons about.

In a dynamic market, operators don't watch vacancy rise to 8% and 
accept it as their stabilized state. They adjust concessions and 
rental rates until lease velocity drives vacancy back toward target. 
The platform's underwriting needs to reflect this operator behavior 
— which means the Cashflow Agent has to reason about:

  - What concession depth (months free, $/month rebates) gets to 5% 
    vacancy given submarket absorption
  - What rent reduction (below asking, below market) is required if 
    concessions alone don't get there
  - The tradeoff: deeper concessions preserve face rent but burn 
    cash; rent reductions are cleaner economically but harder to 
    reverse
  - Operator stance preferences (rent-preservation vs. velocity vs. 
    cash) that determine which strategy fits

This means inputs aren't scalar; concessions and rent become 
temporal profiles keyed to the lease-up curve:

  Months 1-6: 2 months free, asking rent = market - 5%
  Months 7-12: 1 month free, asking rent = market
  Months 13+: no concessions, asking rent = market + 2%

The agent computes the profile that gets to 5% sustained for 12 
months with the operator's preferred tradeoffs.

PART 2 — The correlation engine is the architectural answer

How does the agent reason about the concession-velocity-rent 
relationship? The correlation engine.

The platform has historical observation data — Jacksonville back 
to 2018, two Atlanta properties from 2020 and 2022, plus 
submarket-level historical data from CoStar and Apartment 
Locator. The correlation engine (correlation-metrics-engine.jsx 
and M36-M38) is designed to surface empirical relationships 
between platform-tracked metrics.

The Cashflow Agent queries the correlation engine for historical 
relationships between underwriting levers (concession depth, rent 
positioning) and outcomes (lease velocity, time-to-stabilization), 
gets back coefficients with confidence, and uses that to reason 
about the subject property's required strategy. The agent cites 
"based on Atlanta Midtown's 2020-2025 lease-up data" rather than 
"based on platform default."

This is the platform's analytical thesis made concrete: empirical 
underwriting from accumulated historical observation data, not 
generic AI priors.
